## Part 1 — Benchmark: The UK ISA in 2024

### Market Size & Adoption

The UK Individual Savings Account (ISA) is the reference model for this analysis.
Based on **HMRC Annual Savings Statistics (September 2025)**:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Adult ISA accounts subscribed (2023/24) | ~15 million | HMRC, Sept 2025 |
| Total subscriptions (2023/24) | £103 billion | HMRC, Sept 2025 |
| Year-on-year subscription increase | +£31.4 billion (+44%) | HMRC, Sept 2025 |
| Market value of all Adult ISA holdings | **£872 billion** | HMRC, Sept 2025 |
| YoY increase in market value | +20.1% | HMRC, Sept 2025 |
| Annual contribution allowance | £20,000 per person | HMRC |

The surge in 2023/24 subscriptions was primarily driven by **Cash ISA inflows (+67%,
£27.9 billion)**, directly attributable to elevated Bank of England base rates. This
confirms that ISA adoption is interest-rate sensitive — a critical factor when modelling
European expansion timing.

### Product Split

- **Cash ISA:** 66.2% of accounts subscribed; 41.3% of total market value
- **Stocks & Shares ISA:** minority of accounts; **58.6% of total market value**
- Junior ISA: ~1.37 million accounts subscribed in 2023/24 (£1.8 billion)

### Key ISA Features (for cross-market comparison)

| Feature | UK ISA |
|---|---|
| Tax treatment | Tax-free on income, dividends AND capital gains |
| Annual contribution cap | £20,000 |
| Holding period required for tax benefit | None (immediate) |
| Eligible assets | Cash, equities, funds, bonds, IFISAs |
| Portability | Provider transfer allowed (no tax event) |
| Minimum holding period | None |

The absence of a minimum holding period and the dual tax exemption (income + CGT) are
the most structurally distinctive features of the ISA relative to European equivalents.

## Part 2 — European Comparable Models

### 2.1 France — Plan d'Épargne en Actions (PEA)

**Source:** Banque de France (2024 statistics); European Commission benchmark study
(cited in Gide LLP, 2025); European Commission Recommendation on SIAs (October 2024)

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Total PEA accounts (end 2024) | **7.3 million** |
| Total AuM | **€114 billion** |
| YoY AuM growth | +€1.3 billion (from €112.7Bn in 2023) |
| Annual contribution cap | €150,000 (€225,000 for PEA-PME) |
| Minimum holding for full tax exemption | **5 years** |
| Eligible assets | EU-domiciled equities, UCITS funds |
| Year of creation | 1992 (oldest EU tax-advantaged investment account) |

**Key structural differences vs. ISA:**
- The PEA requires a **5-year lock-up** before full income/CGT exemption applies.
  Early withdrawal triggers full taxation and account closure.
- Coverage universe is restricted to **EU-listed companies** (no US equities, no global
  ETFs unless synthetically structured).
- The European Commission's October 2024 recommendation on Savings and Investment
  Accounts (SIAs) concludes that the PEA satisfies *most* recommended criteria but
  falls short on "most favourable tax treatment available" and flexibility.

**Market entry signal (France):** The PEA market is **mature and embedded**. High
structural friction: client acquisition requires convincing investors to *transfer*
their existing PEA (a process the AMF has repeatedly sanctioned custodians for delaying).
Transfer complaints are among the most frequent filed with the AMF mediator (Gide, 2025).

**Attractiveness for ISA-equivalent expansion: MEDIUM-LOW**
- Established consumer behaviour around PEA limits greenfield opportunity.
- Partnership model (with an existing PEA custodian) more viable than direct entry.
- PEA-PME sub-product targeting SME investment shows growth potential.

### 2.2 Germany — No Wrapper; Surging ETF-Sparplan Market

**Source:** State Street Global Advisors (2025 Global ETF Outlook: Spotlight on Germany);
extraETF European ETF Savings Plan Study (November 2024)

Germany has **no ISA-equivalent tax wrapper**. This is simultaneously the biggest
barrier and the most significant opportunity in this analysis.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| ETF savings plan (Sparplan) accounts | **9.5 million** (end of 2024) |
| YoY growth in ETF Sparpläne | **+34%** (from 7.1 million in 2023) |
| ETF retail investors | 3.5 million (+25% YoY from 2.8 million) |
| Annual ETF savings plan inflows | **€15.6 billion** |
| Projected annual flows by 2028 | **€42 billion** |
| Household savings rate | **>21%** (EU's highest; Eurostat Q4 2024) |

Germany has the **highest household savings rate in the EU** (>21% of gross disposable
income, Eurostat Q4 2024). Yet retail investment participation remains comparatively
low, with savings largely parked in low-yielding bank accounts (*Sparbuch*, *Tagesgeld*).

**The key structural gap:**
- Gains from ETF Sparpläne are subject to the standard German **Abgeltungsteuer**
  (25% flat capital gains tax + solidarity surcharge + church tax where applicable).
- There is no wrapper that shields accumulated gains or dividends from taxation
  comparable to the ISA structure.
- The EU Commission's October 2024 Savings and Investment Account (SIA) Recommendation
  directly targets this gap at member-state level.

**Distribution channel:** Unlike France or the UK (where platforms dominate), German
retail investment runs **through banks** (State Street, 2025). This creates a clear
partnership pathway — any ISA-equivalent product launch in Germany requires bank
distribution deals rather than a D2C platform model.

**Attractiveness for ISA-equivalent expansion: HIGH**
- Largest unaddressed retail savings market in the EU by savings rate.
- Existing behavioural pattern (Sparplan) maps directly onto ISA mechanics.
- Regulatory tailwind from EU SIA Recommendation.
- No incumbent ISA-type product to displace.
- Bank partnerships required but well-defined distribution structure.

### 2.3 Sweden — Investeringssparkonto (ISK)

**Source:** Fondbolagen (Swedish Investment Fund Association), ISK Report October 2024;
Investment Officer (2024/2025); European Commission SIU communications (2025)

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Net savings via ISK accounts (2014–Q2 2024) | **SEK 360 billion** |
| Tax model | Flat "schablonskatt" on portfolio value (not on realised gains) |
| Annual cap | None |
| Eligible assets | Listed equities, funds (broad) |
| Minimum holding period | None |

The ISK is **the closest structural equivalent to the UK ISA** among all EU/EEA markets.
It was introduced in 2012. Rather than exempting gains entirely, it taxes a notional
yield (calculated from the government borrowing rate) applied to the total portfolio
value — **regardless of whether gains are realised**.

**EU relevance:** Both the European Commission and the Dutch fund association DUFAS have
explicitly cited the Swedish ISK as a **model for a pan-European savings account**
(Investment Officer, 2025). The Commission's Savings and Investments Union (SIU)
strategy explicitly references it in its #InvestInYou campaign.

**Attractiveness for ISA-equivalent expansion: MEDIUM**
- Market already has a functioning, widely adopted wrapper — less urgency.
- Positive: consumer familiarity with the concept makes education costs low.
- Barrier: the Swedish market is small (~10 million population); standalone expansion
  may not justify setup costs without Nordics bundling strategy.

## Part 3 — Macro Context: EU Savings and Investments Union

**Source:** European Commission SIU strategy page; Draghi Report (2024);
Eurostat Household Income & Saving Statistics (2025)

| Indicator | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Euro area household savings rate (Q3 2024) | **15.3%** | Eurostat |
| EU household investment rate (2024) | 8.7% of gross HH disposable income | Eurostat |
| Investment gap vs. savings | ~7 percentage points | Eurostat |
| Additional annual investment needed in EU by 2030 | **€750–800 billion** | Draghi Report 2024 |
| EU Commission SIA Recommendation | Adopted October 30, 2024 | EC |

The structural **under-investment of household savings** is now a formal EU policy
priority. The Commission's October 2024 Recommendation calls on member states to
introduce or reform tax-advantaged savings and investment accounts aligned with a common
framework. This regulatory momentum directly benefits any ISA-equivalent product
seeking European expansion.
